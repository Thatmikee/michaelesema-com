His system, ADN-1, deploys synthetic DNA markers onto stolen goods the moment they leave the shelf, making every item permanently traceable and commercially worthless.

Michael Esema was born in Nsit Ubium, Akwa Ibom State, Nigeria, and grew up in Abuja. His mother, Dr. Mary Esema, is an Assistant Director at the National Malaria Elimination Programme within Nigeria's Federal Ministry of Health — a virologist who has spent her career building the public health systems that protect people. His father, the late Barr. Essien Joseph Esema, was a lawyer and politician who served as Senior Special Assistant to the Governor of Akwa Ibom State. Governance and public accountability were the household standard. Michael absorbed that and built something of his own with it.

He studied Accounting at Benson Idahosa University in Benin City, where he was elected President of NUASA, the student body's accounting union. He ran it the way he thought institutions should be run: every naira tracked, every process documented, every gap closed. During his tenure, he hosted the President of the Institute of Chartered Accountants of Nigeria (ICAN) on campus and led the student push for BIU to meet ICAN accreditation standards and secure a Mutual Cooperation Agreement (MCATI). The MOU was signed shortly after, giving BIU accounting graduates 10 ICAN subject exemptions instead of the standard 7. He graduated in 2018.

His first role was at the National Emergency Management Agency (NEMA) in Abuja, working in the Director of Finance and Administration's office. It was supposed to be a starting point. It became an education. He watched how institutional leakage, small and constant, could hollow out an organisation from the inside without anyone raising an alarm.

From NEMA, Michael moved to B's Hive Hotel and Suites in Abuja as Head Accountant, managing departmental budgets of up to ₦10 million a year. He earned his Masters in Business Administration (MBA) from the Nigerian Defence Academy at the same time. While balancing both, he overhauled the hotel's accounting systems, replacing manual processes with structures that actually held up under scrutiny. He was promoted to Assistant Manager, and that is when things got interesting.

As Assistant Manager, Michael didn't just keep the operation running. He modernised it. He identified an underused lounge space and partnered with Silent Party 9ja to launch a silent lounge experience, a format that was new to Abuja's hospitality scene. The events exceeded revenue projections by 75%. A space that was costing the hotel money started making it. That instinct — seeing a dead asset and turning it into a revenue line — would show up again later.

Outside the hotel, Michael was already building. He launched M'S Shoes, a retail venture that grew from local sales into international orders reaching Gambia and Ghana. He learned what it means to manage inventory across borders, deal with supply chain friction, and sell to markets where trust is built face to face, not through a website.

At every position he held, Michael identified the same structural failure playing out differently. Funds leaking at NUASA, transaction records at NEMA, inventory shortfalls at B's Hive. He audited, documented, and closed gaps wherever his authority let him. What he could not fix within his scope, he mapped. The problem was not that nobody noticed the losses — it was that the available tools were built for large retailers with full security departments and six-figure budgets. Nothing existed for the independent shop on a side street. He recognised that gap years before he had the means to close it.

In 2023, Michael Esema moved to Manchester. He enrolled in an MSc in International Business Management at Manchester Metropolitan University and graduated with Merit in 2024. Manchester showed him a version of the same problem he had seen his whole career, only sharper.

Over 530,000 shoplifting offences were recorded in England and Wales in the year to March 2025 — a 20% rise, the highest since records began. The British Retail Consortium puts the cost at over £2.2 billion a year. Convenience stores alone logged 6.2 million theft incidents in twelve months. Over 2,000 incidents of violence against shop workers happen every day. Nearly half of all thefts go unreported because retailers have stopped believing anyone will come.

The chains have entire loss prevention departments. The independent jeweller on the high street has a bell on the door.

Michael saw the gap. He had been seeing it his whole life.

He founded Mykei Securities Ltd in January 2026 and designed the ADN-1 Active Forensic Defence Node from scratch. He taught himself embedded systems programming, wrote the firmware in C/C++, wired up dual Time-of-Flight sensors, built the cloud integration on AWS IoT Core, and assembled a working prototype on his kitchen table. Then he put on a jacket and walked the streets of Manchester, knocking on shop doors, sitting with owners, hearing their stories, and asking them to trust something that did not exist yet. Five of them said yes.

He filed a patent covering 17 claims. He built the company website. He wrote the business plan. He designed the pricing model. Not because he wanted to do everything himself, but because someone had to go first.

The idea behind the ADN-1 is simple. Michael calls it Economic Sterilisation. When someone sweeps a shelf clean, the device detects it and deploys synthetic DNA forensic markers onto the stolen goods. Invisible. Non-toxic. Permanent. Every tagged item is linked to the specific crime, the specific store, and the exact time it happened. The goods become unsellable — not because they are damaged, but because they are now forensic evidence. Theft stops making financial sense.

That discipline runs through every role Michael Esema has held. He tracked student union funds at Benson Idahosa. He reconciled agency accounts at NEMA. He managed hotel inventory at B's Hive. He studied retail theft on the high streets of Manchester. Different countries, different industries, same structural problem. He had been building responses to it his whole career. Mykei Securities is where that work became a company.

He is 29. He has three degrees, a pending patent, and five live pilots. He is not done.

"Most people in security started in technology and found a business problem. Michael started in governance, found a broken system, and built the technology to fix it."

02 The Product

What I'm building

Patent Pending, MYK-PAT-001

ADN-1 Active Forensic Defence Node

Mykei Securities Ltd  ·  Hardware-Enabled SaaS

A shelf-mounted IoT device designed for independent jewellers, off-licences, and convenience stores. Dual VL53L0X Time-of-Flight sensors detect when multiple items are swept from shelves simultaneously, the signature pattern of organised retail crime. Upon detection, the device autonomously deploys a synthetic DNA forensic marker mist onto the stolen goods.

The forensic marker is invisible, non-toxic, and permanent. It links every tagged item to the specific crime event, store, and timestamp. Tagged goods become commercially worthless. Any attempt to resell them creates a forensic trail directly to the handler.
